1. Vowel Sounds
Short Vowels: These are quick, short sounds.
Sound Phonetic Symbol Examples
Short 'a' /√¶/ cat, hat, map, apple
Short 'e' /e/ bed, red, head, many
Short 'i' /…™/ sit, fit, big, win
Short 'o' /…í/ hot, pot, dog, stop
Short 'u' / å/ cup, luck, bus, money
Long Vowels: These are extended sounds, often marked with a colon (:) in phonetic symbols.
Sound Phonetic Symbol Examples
Long 'a' /e…™/ day, say, make, rain
Long 'e' /iÀê/ see, tree, me, people
Long 'i' /a…™/ my, fly, time, like
Long 'o' /…ô ä/ go, no, home, know
Long 'u' /uÀê/ blue, true, food, moon
Pronunciation Tip

When practicing vowel sounds, exaggerate the mouth position at first. For example, really stretch your lips wide for the /iÀê/ sound in "see" and round them significantly for the /uÀê/ sound in "blue".

2. Consonant Sounds
Challenging Consonants: These are sounds that Thai speakers often find difficult.
Sound Phonetic Symbol Examples Common Errors
'th' (voiced) /√∞/ the, this, that, those Often pronounced as 'd'
'th' (unvoiced) /Œ∏/ think, three, mouth, bath Often pronounced as 't'
'v' /v/ very, love, five, have Often confused with 'w'
'z' /z/ zoo, lazy, buzz, zero Often pronounced as 's'
'r' /r/ red, very, sorry, right Different from Thai 'r'
'l' /l/ love, hello, feel, light Different from Thai 'l'
Consonant Clusters: These are groups of consonants that appear together.
  • street, strong, strange
  • splash, split, splendid
  • asks, desks, tasks
  • three, throw, through
Pronunciation Tip

For the 'th' sounds, place the tip of your tongue between your teeth and blow air out. For /Œ∏/ (as in "think"), don't use your voice. For /√∞/ (as in "the"), use your voice.

3. Stress and Intonation
Word Stress: In English, certain syllables in words are emphasized more than others.
  • PHOtograph (stress on first syllable)
  • phoTOgraphy (stress on second syllable)
  • photoGRAphic (stress on third syllable)
  • INterest (stress on first syllable)
  • inTEResting (stress on second syllable)
Sentence Stress: Content words (nouns, verbs, adjectives, adverbs) are usually stressed in sentences, while function words (articles, prepositions, pronouns) are usually unstressed.
  • I WANT to GO to the PARK.
  • She LIVES in a BEAUTIFUL HOUSE.
  • WHAT are you DOING LATER?
Intonation: The rise and fall of voice when speaking.
  • You're coming? (rising intonation - question)
  • You're coming. (falling intonation - statement)
  • Wow! (rising-falling intonation - excitement)
Pronunciation Tip

Listen to native speakers and try to mimic not just the sounds but also the rhythm and melody of their speech. Watching English movies or TV shows with subtitles can help you connect the written words with their natural pronunciation.
